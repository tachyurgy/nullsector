An ode to the programming battle game

Ghosts in the Machine

Sixty-five years of code written to kill code. This is the story of the programming battle game — the oldest game programmers ever played — and of the little ring of memory where two programs enter and one program leaves.

1961 · Darwin 1984 · Core War The Bestiary The Robots The Modern Age Why It Endures

Most games ask you to play them. A programming battle game asks you to write a player — a small autonomous program — and then send it in alone, with no hand on the controls, to fight the programs other people wrote. You don't move a piece. You author a mind, release it, and watch it live or die by the logic you gave it. Win or lose, you learn something true about how you think.

It is a strange and beautiful corner of computing, and an old one. Long before esports, before AI agents, before "self-play," programmers were already staging duels between little machine-code creatures and gathering around a screen to watch. The genre has been reinvented a dozen times — on mainframes, on the Apple II, on the Macintosh, in Java, in the browser — but its heart has never changed: the elegance of a program that must survive on its own.

1961

Darwin, and the first kill

Bell Labs · IBM 7090 · the primordial arena

The story begins in August 1961, in the machine room at Bell Telephone Laboratories, on an IBM 7090 mainframe. Three researchers — Victor Vyssotsky, Robert Morris Sr., and Doug McIlroy — invented a game they called Darwin. (McIlroy would later give the world the Unix pipe; Morris would help write early Unix security, and his son would author the 1988 internet worm — the lineage of this genre runs straight through the lineage of computing itself.)

Darwin was refereed by a program the players called the umpire. It set aside a stretch of memory — the arena — and loaded in small programs the players had written in raw 7090 machine code. Each program was an organism. Through the umpire an organism could probe neighboring memory, try to kill whatever it found there, and claim empty space for copies of itself. Reproduce faster than you are killed and you inherit the arena.

It lasted only a few weeks. Robert Morris studied the dynamics, went home, and came back with an organism nobody could beat — a program so efficient at defending and reproducing that the game simply ended. That is the genre in miniature already: a puzzle that looks open-ended until someone finds the move that closes it, and then the frontier moves on. Darwin was never published as a product. It survived as a story, told and retold — and twenty-three years later, that story lit a fuse.

1984

Core War — the genre gets its name

A. K. Dewdney & D. G. Jones · Redcode · the MARS

In May 1984, the mathematician and computer scientist A. K. Dewdney devoted his "Computer Recreations" column in Scientific American to a game he and D. G. Jones had formalized that spring. They called it Core War, after core — the woven magnetic-ring memory of the mainframe era. Dewdney's column reached hundreds of thousands of readers, and a subculture ignited overnight. Where Darwin was a legend passed between insiders, Core War was a game anyone with a computer could build and play. It gave the genre its vocabulary — and, for the first time, a name.

The setup is deceptively simple. Two programs — now called warriors — are written in an abstract assembly language named Redcode and loaded at random into a single ring of memory. A virtual machine, the MARS (Memory Array Redcode Simulator), runs them one instruction at a time, turn by turn. The memory is circular: in Dewdney's original it held 8000 cells numbered 0 to 7999, and cell 8000 was simply cell 0 again. There are no walls to back into. A warrior wins when its opponent executes an illegal instruction and dies.

An entire discipline grew up inside those constraints. The International Core War Society formed in 1985 to standardize Redcode so warriors could battle across machines. The First International Core War Tournament ran in September 1986 at The Computer Museum in Boston — 31 entries fed into the memory of borrowed office PCs. It was won by a self-replicating warrior called Mice, written by Chip Wendell, who took home a trophy built around a genuine core-memory board salvaged from a CDC 6600. People were, quite literally, competing for a piece of the machine the game was named after.

And the warriors themselves became astonishingly small poems. The most famous is the Imp, Dewdney's own, and it is a single instruction:

; IMP — the immortal. One line. It copies itself
; one cell ahead, forever, crawling around the ring.
        MOV 0, 1     ; copy THIS cell to the next, then step into it

That's the whole program. MOV 0, 1 copies the current cell to the cell just ahead — which is itself — and then execution steps forward into the fresh copy and does it again. The Imp is a wave that walks the entire core and cannot quite be killed, only outpaced. From that one gesture the community derived imp-rings, imp-spirals, and a whole zoo of strategies. Which brings us to the bestiary.

The

A bestiary of warriors

bomber · scanner · replicator · imp · vampire

Because the core is symmetric and blind, no single tactic wins. Instead the archetypes fall into a rock–paper–scissors triangle that Core War players have argued about for forty years: broadly, replicators (Paper) outlast bombers (Stone), bombers shred scanners (Scissors), and scanners pick apart replicators. Master one and you become food for another. Here are the creatures.

The Bomber

Stone

Sits still and rains DAT mines across the ring at a fixed stride, hoping the enemy walks into one. Small, fast, merciless — the oldest offensive pattern, seeded by Dewdney's original Dwarf.

The Scanner

Scissors

Refuses to bomb blindly. It sweeps memory looking for the enemy's signature, and only when it finds a live target does it strike that exact address. Patient and precise — it beats programs that sprawl.

The Replicator

Paper

Copies itself all over the core, so that killing one instance barely matters — there are dozens more. Hard to eradicate, it wins by sheer persistence. Chip Wendell's Mice was the first champion of this school.

The Imp

The immortal

A single MOV 0, 1 that walks the ring copying itself forever. Nearly unkillable, rarely a winner alone — but woven into imp-rings it becomes a defensive backbone that forces ties out of losses.

The Vampire

Pit-trap

Doesn't kill outright — it plants JMP traps that hijack the enemy's execution and drag it into a captive loop of the vampire's making. Turn your opponent's own instruction pointer against them.

The Turtle

Denial

Wins by not losing. It hardens a small footprint, hides, and runs out the clock — forcing a draw against attackers it can't beat and stealing points from the reckless. In a ladder, a tie you engineer is a loss you refused.

1981

The robots leave the core

RobotWar · CRobots · Robocode

Core War kept the fight abstract — code against code in bare memory. A parallel tradition asked a friendlier question: what if your program drove a robot? Same idea, new metaphor. You still write autonomous logic and set it loose with no controls; now it has a body, a gun, and a radar, and you watch it roll around an arena.

1981

RobotWar

Born on the PLATO network and published by Muse Software for the Apple II. You programmed a tank in a simple language and pitted it against others — the first robot-combat programming game to reach home computers.

1985

CRobots

Tom Poindexter's shareware classic. Robots were programmed in a stripped-down dialect of C — for many hobbyists, the first time writing real-looking code felt like play. Spawned CROBOTS clones for decades.

2001

Robocode

Mathew Nelson's open-source hit, released through IBM. You write a battle tank in Java, and it fights in real time. It became a beloved way to learn Java — programming games as a teaching tool, at scale.

These games did something quietly important: they turned a niche mainframe pastime into a way to learn to program. A teenager who would never touch Redcode might spend a weekend teaching a Robocode tank to circle-strafe — and come away, without noticing, fluent in loops, state, and events. The battle was the bait; the programming was the point.

Today

The modern age

browsers · assembly puzzles · AI arenas

The genre never died — it kept finding new hosts. Cheap compute and the browser let it explode outward into whole new shapes, from persistent multiplayer worlds to meticulous assembly puzzles to the AI-agent arenas that now train the frontier.

2015

TIS-100 & the Zachtronics school

Zachtronics turned "the assembly language game you never asked for" into a genre of its own — TIS-100, Shenzhen I/O, EXAPUNKS. Not combat, but the same joy: an austere machine, a hard constraint, an elegant program.

2016

Screeps

A persistent MMO where your only interface is JavaScript. Your colony keeps running on the server whether you're watching or asleep — Core War's autonomy stretched across a living, always-on world.

MIT

Battlecode

MIT's storied AI programming competition. Teams code armies that fight in a real-time strategy engine — a direct academic heir to the "write an agent, set it loose, may the best logic win" tradition.

web

CodinGame & Leek Wars

Browser arenas where thousands submit bot code to climb bot-vs-bot leaderboards, in a dozen languages. The tournament ladder of 1986, now global and always open.

2019

Bitburner & hackmud

Idle/hacking games where scripting is the gameplay — you automate your way to power in JavaScript. The programming isn't a metaphor for the game; it's the whole loop.

now

Self-play & agent arenas

The deepest echo: modern AI is trained by pitting agents against copies of themselves and keeping what wins. Darwin's core idea — release autonomous programs, let survival do the judging — turns out to be how machines now learn.

Every one of these is the same wish, restated for a new decade: write a mind, let it go, and find out if it was any good.

§

Why it endures

the ode

Why has this one idea kept coming back for sixty-five years, when whole genres of games have been born and buried in the meantime? A few reasons, and they are worth loving.

It is honest. Once your warrior is loaded, you cannot help it. No reflexes, no clutch save, no second-guessing the joystick. Every good thing that happens was your foresight; every death was your blind spot, revealed in public. Few games hold up so clean a mirror to how you actually think.

It is deterministic, so it is fair. Give the machine the same two programs and the same starting seed and it produces the same battle every time — which means a result can be re-checked, a bug can be reproduced, and a victory can never be luck you can't account for. The whole drama is a pure function of the code. That is a rare and beautiful property for a game to have.

It is watchable. This is the quiet magic. Abstract code becomes a moving picture — pointers streaking around a ring, mines blooming, a replicator flooding the core while a scanner hunts it cell by cell. You are, in the most literal sense, watching a program think. Programmers spend their lives imagining execution in their heads; here it is on the screen, dancing.

And it is bottomless. Twelve instructions, or twenty, and no one ever runs out of ideas. Darwin ended in weeks because one arena had one answer; the genre as a whole never ends, because every time someone builds the perfect predator, someone else builds the thing that eats it. It is evolution staged in silicon, and evolution, famously, does not stop.

2026 · this game

Where NULLSECTOR stands in the line

NULLSECTOR is a deliberately approachable descendant of Core War, built for the browser. It keeps the sacred parts — one ring of memory, addresses relative to where you are right now, and a single rule for death — and throws out the cruelty: no multiprocessing, no addressing-mode soup, just one walker, four registers, and twelve readable instructions. Write a daemon, watch it fight, and duel the ghosts of every pilot who came before you.
